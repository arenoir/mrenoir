RECOGNITION AS RESILIENCE: HOW AN UNRECOGNIZED INDIGENOUS NATION IS USING VISIBILITY AS A PATHWAY TOWARDS RESTORATIVE JUSTICE

Megan Renoir1*, Shelly Covert2, Siad Darwish3

1 Department of History, University of Cambridge, UK

2 Nevada City Rancheria Nisenan Tribe, USA

3 CDA Collaborative Learning, USA; University of Melbourne, Australia

*Corresponding author: mdr64@cam.ac.uk

Abstract

Resilience has been conceptualized within international development studies as the ability of individuals or groups to ‘return to normality’ after environmental, social, and/or economic shocks. Given the chronic adversity and systematic oppression faced by Indigenous communities in the United States, how can we make sense of the idea of a return to normal when it is not a return to their normal? The Nevada City Rancheria Nisenan Tribe (NCRNT), an unrecognized Indigenous nation in Northern California, face ongoing challenges to its sovereign status within federal borders, and by proxy, to its survival. A key challenge to NCRNT resilience stems from the Tribe’s historic erasure caused by genocide, as well illegal termination and dispossession of its land bases and political influence vis-à-vis local, state and federal government agencies. Because of this history, the NCRNT has defined its previous resilience strategy as mere ‘survival,’ reflecting the Tribe’s internal efforts to protect its culture and people throughout the latter half of the twentieth century. In cases such as this then, how can unrecognized Indigenous nations forge forms of resilience that are reflective of their normal? One vector for this possibility is through the production of information about their past and present that is visible to a world of policymakers, which serves to highlight the value and significance of Indigenous histories on both the domestic and global stage. Within the last fifteen years the NCRNT have undertaken efforts to increase its visibility that have produced critical pathways towards increased autonomy, agency and ultimately resilience. Using recent oral histories, artwork, and documentation from the newly created NCRN Tribal archive, this paper explores the history of the tribe and the ways in which their mode of resilience has transformed in the twenty first century from a strategy survival to a conscious pursuit of visibility. By engaging with Indigenous histories and experiences, this article challenges the underlying assumption of normality within international development concepts of resilience and associated practice. Rather than a return to a presumed normal, the NCRNT’s visibility efforts in the twenty-first century reframe the concept of resilience to link the political past, present and future together in rhetoric and action to meet the Tribe’s needs. The NCRNT’s experience reveals how activities underlying Indigenous resilience represent a need for historical redress and reconciliation, thereby creating a ‘new normal’ that is reflective of Native history as well as the ongoing social, political, and economic realities of existing within a settler state. This exploration of resilience expands the dialectical possibilities of history and development studies and provides historical purchase to ongoing efforts of Indigenous groups seeking restorative justice. Introduction

Resilience has been conceptualized within international development studies as the ability of individuals or groups to ‘return to normality’ after environmental, social, and/or economic shocks. [1] Given the chronic adversity and systematic oppression faced by Indigenous communities in the United States, how can we make sense of the idea of a return to normal when it is not a return to their normal? The Nevada City Rancheria Nisenan Tribe (NCRNT), an unrecognized Indigenous nation in Northern California, face ongoing challenges to its sovereign status within federal borders, and by proxy, to its survival. A key challenge to NCRNT resilience stems from the Tribe’s historic erasure caused by genocide, as well illegal termination and dispossession of its land bases and political influence vis-à-vis local, state, and federal government agencies. Because of this history, the NCRNT has defined its previous resilience strategy as mere ‘survival,’ reflecting its internal efforts to protect NCRNT culture and people throughout the latter half of the twentieth century. In cases such as this then, how can unrecognized Indigenous nations forge forms of resilience that are reflective of their normal? One vector for this possibility is through the production of information about their past and present that is visible to a world of policymakers, which serves to highlight the value and significance of Indigenous histories on both the domestic and global stage. Within the last fifteen years the NCRNT have undertaken efforts to increase its visibility that have produced critical pathways towards increased autonomy, agency and ultimately resilience. In effect, the NCRNT has transformed its resilience strategy in the twenty-first century from a strategy of survival to a conscious pursuit of visibility that frames restorative justice as a necessary feature of effective resilience.

The NCRNT’s contemporary approach to resilience was made clear on December 18, 2022, when members of the Tribe gathered with non-Native community members for a workshop focused on identifying the ‘key driving factors for peace, conflicts, climate vulnerabilities, and resilience’ in Nevada County, California. The workshop was hosted by CDA Collaborative Learning, an international development organization that conducts participatory research dedicated to improving the effectiveness of global peacebuilding, development and humanitarian efforts. [2] As part of CDA’s global project on the ‘ Environment-Fragility-Peace Nexus,’ the workshop aimed to develop lessons on Indigenous resilience strategies to inform policy and practice within international development. [3] CDA sought out the NCRNT for collaboration due to the Tribe’s position as an Indigenous group operating in a region of the United States that experiences recurring environmental disasters. Specifically, California’s history of fire suppression policies beginning in the 1800s have created a modern wildfire crisis. [4] As of 2023, approximately fifty percent of the Nevada County population live in areas labeled as very high wildfire-risk.’ [5] Catastrophic megafires have become a year-round threat, and the state’s most destructive and deadliest fires have all occurred in the last decade. [6] In addition to wildfires, the region experiences high levels of environmental toxicity due to the history of hydraulic gold mining from 1848 to the 1940s when Nevada County was one of the most productive gold mining regions in the country. [7]

Organizations like CDA have grown increasingly interested in collaborating with Indigenous groups to inform normative resilience practice against such environmental risks. [8] Indigenous groups represent one of the most vulnerable populations impacted by conflict and climate change. [9] They are also the primary population associated with adaptive environmental strategies that have proven effective in preventing and responding to environmental vulnerabilities historically. [10] The NCRNT are therefore approached frequently by state, federal, non-profit and private actors to inform practical strategies that facilitate resilience against environmental risks such as wildfires and environmental toxicity {% view_video NCRNT-council-elders-in conversation-with-tribal-spokesperson-shelly-covert %}. Requests tend to focus on the Tribe’s history of employing traditional burns to reduce the scale and intensity of wildfires and sustainably manage local ecological systems. This relationship between Indigenous vulnerability, traditional ecological knowledge and climate change resilience informed CDA’s interest in approaching the NCRNT for research collaboration in 2022. Through the research, CDA hoped to identify how traditional ecological knowledge could be employed for conflict and climate change resilience for communities that face similar challenges as the NCRNT. Yet, as is often the case with participatory research, the discussions at CDA’s workshop produced a nuanced concept of resilience that moved beyond development ideas of easily transferable practices related to wildfires or environmental toxicity. By the end of the workshop, members of the NCRNT, non-Native community members, and CDA researchers agreed on the following conclusion:

“Contemporary climate vulnerabilities experienced in California are directly tied to the state’s history of genocide against Indigenous people. Adapting to and building resilience against these vulnerabilities thereby necessitates addressing historical injustices that cause these vulnerabilities.” [11]

The workshop produced a historically centered concept of resilience that linked political and environmental vulnerabilities together, and posited restorative justice as a necessary feature of effective resilience. {% view_video CDA_workshop_factors_for_resilience %} This conceptual re-framing of resilience marks a significant departure from normative apolitical and ahistorical concepts within the development field. Development discourse has traditionally prioritized the concept of resilience as a ‘return to normal’ and neglected the role of self-determination both in identifying how and why Indigenous groups are resilient, to what they are resilient against, and what the ‘end goal’ of such resilience might be. What is missing from these concepts is the fact that Indigenous experiences and definitions of resilience are inherently tied to issues of ‘power, knowledge, justice and self-determination.’ [12] In exploring the NCRNT’s participation in various visibility efforts such as the development of a support non-profit; the creation of a Tribal archive; and various research collaborations such as CDA’s workshop, this digital article highlights the necessity of reframing international development theory and practice on resilience to address historical oppression and trauma.

Part I: “Indigenous resilience” in International Development

Originating in the field of ecology during the 1960s and 1970s, [13] the concept of resilience refers to “the time required for a system to return to an equilibrium or steady-state following a perturbation.” [14] Central to this definition is the assumption that natural systems operate in a state of equilibrium, which they can return to following extreme events given the right conditions. This underlying logic of resilience as a ‘return to normal’ transferred into social discourse through the adoption of the concept within development studies. In the 1980s, the concept of resilience expanded beyond its origins in ecology into social systems. Researchers began examining how social groups adapt to stresses and rapid changes, and eventually linked social resilience to sustainable development and reducing the vulnerability of communities. By the 1990s and early 2000s, the field of development studies adopted resilience thinking, applying it to issues of poverty, disasters, climate change, and conflict. [15] Resilience moved from a descriptive concept to being seen as a normative goal for development programs, with the aim of building capacities to help communities better absorb shocks and shorten ‘recovery’ times. Today, the United Nations defines resilience as “the ability of a system, community or society exposed to hazards to resist, absorb, accommodate, adapt to, transform and recover from the effects of a hazard in a timely and efficient manner.” [16] Development concepts of resilience thus reflect their roots in ecology by focusing on the ability of communities to ‘bounce back’ after crises.

The emphasis of returning to normality within development definitions of resilience takes a short-term view that obscures the impacts of complex histories of injustice and subjugation against communities such as the NCRNT. [17] Following two centuries of genocidal policies and ongoing structural constraints to their sovereign rights, the NCRNT has only 66 enrolled members remaining today, 87 percent of whom live at or below the poverty line. [18] In the context of fundamentally altered cultural, political, economic, and environmental realities, the NCRNT therefore have no ‘normal’ from which to return. {% view_video NCRNT_Elder_Virginia_Covert %} The experience of the NCRNT reflects a broader critique amongst scholars who have argued that international development concepts of resilience represent forms of contemporary violence and colonialism by neglecting such histories of oppression. [19] A prominent critique in this regard is Mark Pelling and David Manuel-Navarrete’s argument that resilience approaches ignore root causes of inequality by focusing on returning societies to an ‘imagined stable state.’ [20] They argue that resilience analysis focuses on system preservation and restoration, neglecting issues of power, politics and justice. This treats social change as pathological rather than organic and suggests disaster responses should aim to return to the pre-disaster status quo. Pelling and Manuel-Navarrete further contend that resilience discourse makes vulnerable communities responsible for their own security without addressing root causes or transforming underlying conditions. By retaining a focus on stabilizing pre-existing social and ecological conditions, this approach effectively neglects chronic vulnerabilities, thereby sustaining an unjust “normality” for marginalized groups. [21] Robin May Schott leverages a similar critique of the ahistorical and apolitical nature of resilience. Schott argues that resilience frameworks promote a future orientation that obscures past harms, unfinished political struggles, and the root causes of chronic vulnerabilities. By treating vulnerability as an abstract condition to be overcome through adaptability, resilience ignores how systemic oppression and historical violence produce the very conditions of such vulnerabilities. By disconnecting vulnerability from specific contested political relations and material conditions, resilience also decontextualizes and depoliticizes injury and suffering. [22]

These debates intersect with wider discussions on the politics of recognition and refusal regarding how societies validate identities and what implications this has on social justice, colonialism, and Indigenous sovereignty. [23] The politics of recognition, as theorized by scholars like Charles Taylor and Nancy Fraser, refers to the practice whereby marginalized or disenfranchised groups seek acknowledgment and validation from dominant power structures. [24] In the context of Indigenous rights, this involves seeking recognition of Indigenous identities, cultures, and rights from the state. [25] Recognition can occur through such processes as treaty-making, acknowledgement of land rights, protection of cultural practices, or the establishment of political autonomy, for example through federal recognition in the United States. [26] Recognition can provide material benefits and protections to Indigenous communities and implies a demand for justice, a call for the restoration of rights that were usurped, and an assertion of the Indigenous people's distinct identities and values. [27]

However, critics of recognition like Elizabeth Povinelli, Heidi Sinevaara-Niskanen and Marjo Lindroth note that recognition often comes with conditions set by the settler state, which can limit Indigenous sovereignty and perpetuate colonial control. [28]  Recognition is a complex process with inherent challenges. It often requires Indigenous communities to align with the norms and definitions set by the colonizing state or dominant society to be acknowledged, which might involve compromising their own customs, values, or systems of self-governance. Thus, recognition can potentially become a form of control or assimilation, perpetuating the power dynamics of colonization. The politics of refusal, on the other hand, represents a rejection of the state's authority to define and recognize Indigenous identity and rights. This approach, as articulated by scholars like Audra Simpson and Glen Coulthard, asserts that Indigenous sovereignty exists inherently and independently of state recognition. [29] Rather than seeking validation or acceptance from colonial structures, Indigenous communities assert their autonomy by choosing how, when, and by whom they are recognized. Coulthard argues for a form of Indigenous self-determination that rejects the politics of recognition in favor of refusal, asserting that Indigenous rights and identities exist inherently, regardless of whether they are recognized by the state. [30] Similarly, Simpson posits that the act of refusal can serve as a potent form of resistance against colonial domination. [31] Refusal is therefore predicated on rejecting state procedures or making Indigenous struggle legible to settler law and discourse.

While the politics of refusal offers an enticing politically conscious alternative to the colonial politics of recognition, refusal is not always a feasible option. For large Indigenous nations such as the Mohawk, the politics of refusal provides a genuine opportunity to construct recognition and autonomy, and therefore resilience, beyond the settler state. [32] Yet, in the case of structurally marginalized, small, geographically displaced unrecognized nations like the NCRNT, the politics of refusal may result in the sacrifice of resources and tools that are essential to their resilience and to holding the settler state accountable. [33] Bruce Granville Miller notes how nonrecognition leads to the marginalization of Indigenous groups, as they are denied access to rights and resources that come with state recognition. [34] They are also often excluded from discussions and decisions about their ancestral lands and resources. Nonrecognition thereby contributes to the ongoing dispossession and displacement Indigenous groups, and as such, recognition and visibility may represent a vital resource for resilience. The NCRNT has little choice but to engage with the settler systems to ensure their own survival. As such, we must look beyond theory on the politics of refusal and recognition to better understand the lived-reality and implications of the resilience of small unrecognized nations. Groups like the NCRNT often employ a combination of both the politics of recognition and refusal, seeking state resources and legitimacy without being wholly legible to state power. [35] Recognition politics can therefore remain strategically useful for marginalized groups. [36]

Part II: Resilience of The Nevada City Rancheria Nisenan Tribe in the Nineteenth and Twentieth Century

As of 2023, the Nevada City Rancheria Nisenan Tribe is a federally unrecognized tribe based in Nevada City, California - a small well-known ‘gold rush town’ on the western slopes of the Sierra Nevada mountains. {% view_image western-slope-of-the-sierra-nevada-mountains.png %} The NCRNT has lived in Nevada County since time immemorial and represent one of the political and governmental units within the larger Nisenan Nation. Within the wider Nisenan-speaking whole, members of the NCRNT represent three different and unique dialects of language and distinct territories directly attached to their political and governmental leadership. Prior to the California gold rush, anthropologists and Nisenan Elders say that several thousands of Nisenan occupied the region of California connecting the Sacramento River, the Consumnes River, the crest of the Sierra Nevada mountains, and the North Fork of the ‘Uba (Yuba) River from the Sierra Crest to the Sacramento Valley. [37] The NCRNT represent one of the most vulnerable populations to social and environmental risks in Nevada County. They are one of several remaining tribes in California to have federal services terminated under the 1958 California Rancheria Termination Act and have not seen their federal recognition restored, leaving them without a land base or political sovereignty vis-a-vis the United States. Importantly, denial of federal recognition deprives tribes of freedom of religious practice, repatriation of cultural artifacts, federal health, housing, education and job assistance programs, thus representing an ongoing form of institutional erasure. [38] In many cases denial of these forms of support has kept Indigenous populations in cycles of poverty and scarcity, perpetrating a form of long-term ‘slow’ violence against groups. [39] Tribal members have also been directly affected by wildfires, including the direct loss of housing and property and the experience of annual displacement due to wildfire evacuations. The current status of the NCRNT thereby reflects a long history of Indigenous oppression, dispossession and disenfranchisement by the U.S. government.

Tribes in California such as the NCRNT have faced a particular history of complex institutionalized violence stemming first from the era of Spanish missionization and then from the policies and practices of the Mexican and American governments. At the start of the California gold rush in 1848 and into the early twentieth century, U.S. Federal Indian policy prioritized assimilation via the forced removal of Indigenous people, concentration camps and violence, including murder, and destruction of culture, food sources and livelihoods. The 1850 California Act for the Government and Protection of Indians established the organized stealing of Native children from their families and the placement of them in white custody, effectively creating a generation of Native Nations removed from their traditional lands, languages, and cultures. [40] In 1851 California’s first Governor stated that: “a war of extermination will continue to be waged between the races until the Indian race becomes extinct . . . ,” and, by 1863, fueled by the increasing demand of whites for land and resources, the killing of Indigenous people in California was directly sanctioned by the state and promoted via financial incentives. [41] Policies included the funding of state sponsored “scalp bounties,” whereby private citizens were provided five dollars for every Indian scalp delivered. [42] By 1870, the Native population in California had been reduced to around 30,000 people, less than twenty percent of the population assumed to have existed in 1846. [43] Alongside many other tribes, the Nisenan also faced particularly gendered forms of violence. Nisenan men were specifically targeted and killed to enable the rape and enslavement of Nisenan women, and children were forcibly removed, thus interfering with the reproductive future of the Tribe. [44] Throughout this period, the NCRNT faced extreme population loss – by 1867, only eighteen years after the start of the gold rush, the Tribe had been reduced by roughly ninety-three percent. [45]

Other policies aimed at extermination and assimilation focused on the social, cultural, economic, and political disenfranchisement of Native Americans. The reservation system was used by the U.S. government in California to remove Native groups and open land for maximum settlement during the gold rush. [46] Explicit reservation policies included the government-sanctioned spread of Christianity; the replacement of communal economies with private property institutions; the enforcement of English-only western pedagogy via boarding schools; the control and restriction of Indigenous cultural and religious practices; the granting of US citizenship; and the implementation of US-government managed tribal ‘self-governance.’ [47] Particularly damaging to the resilience and continuity of the NCRNT over time, in 1951, federal agents forcibly removed many of the NCRNT children, taking them to boarding schools and/or placing them in non-Indian foster homes {% view_image NCRNT-children-in-boarding-schools.png %}. [48] Richard Johnson, the current Chairman of the NCRNT, was removed and forced into foster care at age two. {% view_image chairman-of-NCRNT-richard-johnson.png %}

Wildfire suppression policies were damaging to both Indigenous cultural survival and the natural environment. At the start of the gold rush, the U.S. government outlawed native burning practices, in part to preserve grasslands for settler livestock grazing. [49] Fire is integral to Nisenan society and ecology in multiple ways. As a cremation society, they conduct elaborate funeral rituals called ‘cries’ where the deceased and their possessions are burned on pyres, allowing their spirits to enter the afterlife and ascend to the milky way via the Nisenan sacred mountain, ‘Estom Yanim. [50] Prior to the gold rush, the Nisenan also utilized fire to care for the land. Burning helped limit destructive wildfires by reducing fuel loads, controlled harmful insect populations impacting food sources, and enriched soils to improve future grass harvests. [51] Fire suppression thereby enacted colonial violence by disrupting Nisenan land caretaking, severing spiritual relationships to fire, and prohibiting open-air funeral pyres. Today, forests in the Sierra Nevada are highly homogenous, dominated by fire-intolerant species and overly dense with small trees and shrubs that are prone to high-intensity fire. [52] Requests from U.S. government agencies to support with reintroducing traditional burning practices in California therefore land with a sting of historical irony and erasure for the NCRNT. While the NCRNT has remained resilient to environmental shocks historically, European colonization and the banning of Native cultural practices including traditional wildfire prevention created many of the ongoing conditions of vulnerability that demand resilience today. In other words, after over 150 years of attempted erasure of Indigenous groups and culture in California, the same groups that were once targets are now sought for their support in tackling the consequences of the very actions that disposed them in the first place. Worse yet, while U.S. government agencies often request information and knowledge about traditional practices from Indigenous groups, the federal government refuses to acknowledge the political legitimacy of the NCRNT through federal recognition. [53] This neglect for the political reality of Indigenous groups leaves those like the NCRNT in an impossible, one-sided relationship whereby the U.S. government celebrates and extracts traditional knowledge yet ignores and undermines sources of Indigenous self-determination that offer significant support for resilience.  

Relative to other states, tribes in California face particular structural challenges to their attempts at gaining federal recognition due to the state’s history of undermining institutional recognition for Native Americans. In 1851 Nisenan Chief Weimar signed the Camp Union Treaty, which the U.S. Senate rejected along with all other California treaties at the urging of the California congressional delegation in 1852 {% view_image nisenan-chief-weimar-and-california-congressional-delegation.png %}. Treaties would later become an essential component of tribal recognition applications and land reclamation requests in the twentieth and twenty-first century. [54] Legal interpretations for claims are dependent on the United States ratifying treaties, thus establishing a historic record of a government-to-government relationship with tribes. In the absence of ratified treaties, many tribes in California are viewed as non-existent in the eyes of the federal government today. [55] Yet for seventy-three years, the U.S. federal government did in fact recognize the political sovereignty of the NCRNT. In 1891, a member of the Tribe successfully applied for a land allotment through an amendment to the Dawes Severalty Act. Referred to at the time as the ‘Nevada or Colony Tribe of Indians,’ members of the Tribe thus received their first legally protected plot of land under U.S. law. In 1913, following a series of repeated competing land claims to the allotment, President Woodrow Wilson established the allotment as an executive order reservation. {% view_image president-woodrow-wilson-executive-order.png %} Referred to in the executive order as the ‘Nevada or Colony Tribe of Indians,’ members of the Tribe thus received their first legally protected plot of land under U.S. law {% view_image tribe-first-legally-protected-plot-of-land.png %}. The status of the Tribe was reinforced again in 1934, when Tribal members voted to organize under the Indian Reorganization Act (IRA). [56] And in 1947, the Tribe was listed in the Haas Report, which reported on the federally acknowledged tribes organized by the IRA. [57]

In the 1930s, driven by a report of the dire conditions of reservations and Native welfare that challenged emerging U.S. liberal ideals of economic opportunity, federal Indian policy shifted towards a preference for Tribal sovereignty over assimilation. [58] The 1934 Indian Reorganization Act (IRA/Indian New Deal), reorganized tribes as corporate entities in hopes of enabling economic development and Native self-government. [59] While the ideals and many of the outcomes of the IRA were supportive of Tribal self-determination, the IRA also led to diverse and sometimes problematic outcomes for tribes. One such outcome included the codifying of tribal identities in line with Western notions of identity that bifurcated Tribal relationships with land and encouraged isolation from neighboring tribes that had historically shared their ancestry and culture. [60] In the case of the NCRNT, the Nevada City Nisenan identity was legally separated from other Nisenan groups in the region, thus placing strict requirements on who could be considered a Nevada City Nisenan. The Tribe’s participation in the IRA codified this bifurcation. Counterintuitively, the U.S. government neglected the bifurcation of Tribal identity and land in 1958 when Congress voted terminate the Nevada City Rancheria. In a series of policies referred to as ‘termination’ throughout the 1950s and 1960s, the U.S. federal government sold reservation lands and severed the sovereignty of tribes by formally cancelling their federal recognition status. [61] In 1958, Congress named the Nevada City Rancheria in the California Rancheria Act, under which two members of the Tribe’s 22 adult members voted in favor of a distribution plan to sell the 75-acre rancheria {% view_image distribution-of-the-land-and-assets.png %}. As a result, on September 21, 1964 the Nevada City Rancheria land was sold by the federal government via auction to non-Native individuals and the federal trust relationship with the Tribe was terminated. {% view_image termination-of-federal-supervision-article.png %} [62] Today, the NCRNT is treated as an unrecognized tribe by the United States and enjoys no legal right to Tribal land or political sovereignty. [63] The Tribe has been seeking the restoration of their sovereign status, through both formal and informal means, ever since.

It is unsurprising then that when asked how they would have defined their resilience strategy in the twentieth century, Tribal members provided a response reflective of the complete destruction of their social, political, and cultural fabric. Amid ongoing direct and structural violence, Tribe members had no choice but to hide from the dominant society, afraid that speaking their language, conducting traditional practices, or even being identified as Indian would result in punishment such as kidnapping or death. [Saxon video 02 – 5:04] And following the complete loss of their land rights and political sovereignty via termination, Tribal members were left geographically dispersed, economically and political disenfranchised, and culturally damaged. For the NCRNT, the concept of resilience during such a period could only be defined as ‘survival.’

Part III: Seeking a ‘New Normal’ as Resilience in the Twenty-First Century

Despite its historical trajectory, the NCRNT has shown remarkable resilience throughout the twentieth and twenty-first century. By the time of the Tribe’s termination in 1964, their enrolled members stood at only seventeen individuals. Today, the NCRNT has grown to 66 enrolled members and the Tribe has managed to begin successfully rebuilding many of the political and cultural institutions that they saw damaged through colonization. [64] They have also remained politically active in seeking the restoration of their federally recognized status. In the 1970s, the Tribe was a plaintiff in Tillie Hardwick v. U.S. (1979), in which the United States entered into a stipulated judgment that restored federal recognition to Tribes in California that has been wrongly terminated in the 1958 California Rancheria Act. [65] However, the United States omitted the Tribe from the stipulated judgment due to a ‘clerical error.’ [66] The NCRNT then sued the U.S. government in 2010 to correct the Hardwick judgment, but the United States claimed the case had passed the statute of limitations. [67] As a result of these judgments, the NCRNT remain landless and without U.S. recognized political sovereignty, which has led to the Tribe seeking both formal recognition and informal recognition via their ongoing visibility activities.

Today, the NCRNT define their resilience as “the ability to restore, protect and perpetuate Nisenan culture,” emphasizing the safeguarding and transmission of their culture to future generations, the preservation of tribal members' physical well-being, restoration and protection of the natural environment, and the cultivation of historical reconciliation with the non-Native community. [68] [Sarah and Shelly video 04 – 2:09 – 4:37; Ginger 5:53; …]  The NCRNT view the restoration of federal recognition as crucial to achieving these objectives. But meeting the prerequisites for federal recognition and acquiring the necessary financial, human, and political resources creates tensions with Indigenous sovereignty in the interim. Faced with a choice of allocating vital financial and human resources toward federal recognition efforts versus allocating resources to self-recognition activities like language revitalization, curriculum development, and sustaining livelihoods, the NCRNT operate “in both worlds.” [69] While they continue to strive for the restoration of their federal recognition status, the NCRNT has successfully cultivated resilience through the advancement of self-determination in its absence.  As a small unrecognized nation, NCRNT self-determination is predicated on a two-pronged approach: 1) raising visibility of their culture and knowledge within and amongst the Tribe itself; and 2) raising visibility of their continued existence and of the value and significance of their history amongst settler communities and policymakers, locally, nationally, and abroad. Resilience for NCRNT is thus driven by a fusion of both the politics of recognition and refusal. Here, we present three examples of NCRNT visibility efforts that have served to underpin the Tribe’s resilience and transform their political reality both within and beyond the purview of formal U.S. state systems.

a.  Employing a Non-Profit in Lieu of Legal Recognition

In 2014, the NCRNT established the California Heritage: Indigenous Research Project (CHIRP), a 501(c)(3) non-profit guided by the Nevada City Rancheria Tribal Council. The goal of CHIRP is to “research, document and preserve the history and culture of the Nevada City Rancheria Nisenan Tribe.” [70] As a small, unrecognized Indigenous nation, the NCRNT face significant challenges in forging legitimacy as within a settler state. The termination of their federal recognition in 1964 not only severed their federal trust relationship, but also severed opportunities for the Tribe to engage with legal structures for rights claims. The absence of federal recognition also created symbolic illegitimacy for the Tribe. A significant challenge for NCRNT resilience has therefore existed around raising visibility of the Tribe’s continued existence to reestablish this legitimacy. By creating CHIRP, the NCRNT created a formal, public-facing legal entity that is able to “creatively mimic” the symbolic, material and political benefits of federal recognition. [71]

The benefits of CHIRP have been numerous. CHIRP activities have led to the revitalization of the Nisenan language, the development of academic curriculums for local schools, the development of a successful ongoing funding source (the Ancestral Homelands Reciprocity Program), and the ability of the Tribe to partner with other institutions for land management and land return efforts. A significant part of this success has come from CHIRP’s creation of a free public museum ‘Uba Seo and its corresponding annual Visibility Through Art (VTA) program, which has functioned as a primary source of local visibility and political legitimacy for the NCRNT.  Visibility Through Art was inspired by the Tribe’s desire to collaborate with local artists to create artwork that represents and highlights the Nisenan and their “rich but nearly forgotten history here in Nevada County.” [72] Through the program, non-Native artists are “asked to engage in deep listening to the stories and history shared with them by the Tribal members, Tribal Council and Nisenan Elders.” [73] Following Tribal consultation, artists then collaborate individually with Tribal members on art pieces to create reflective, historically and politically engaged works that are then displayed for the public at ‘Uba Seo. Because many of the collaborating artists are non-Nisenan, the program requires a continuous navigation of the sensitive and highly political topics of cultural appropriation, privilege, and erasure. This political and historical engagement with the non-Native community is where the success of ‘Uba Seo and VTA lies. By bringing Native and non-Native community members together to reflect on the NCRNT’s past, present and future through art, ‘Uba Seo and VTA enable the broader community to engage with Nisenan history and ultimately “reflect on the future, navigating a collective conversation.” [74]  

Moreover, VTA exhibits are not just freely available to residents and visitors of Nevada City. By creating virtual tours of exhibits, the Tribe has also made the legitimization of its history accessible and visible to global communities. The significance of this visibility approach should not be underestimated – it is highly unusual for groups as small and under resourced as the NCRNT to develop such strong levels of visibility in the absence of resources and privileges afforded through federal recognition. [75]  From 2021 to 2022, the ‘ Erased’ exhibit focused on the Tribe’s history of legislative and legal erasure. Erased constituted the first time that the Nisenan perspective on local history had been publicly reviewed and discussed in Nevada City. The exhibit featured six physical sections in the gallery, each highlighting different aspects of Nisenan history, culture, and erasure, thus highlighting anti-Indian legislation, the negation of Tribal sovereignty, and the lasting impacts of forced assimilation that continue to affect the Tribe. [76] In 2023, the ‘hamukum exhibit – Nisenan for “. . .they said long ago. . .” – counters settler narratives of the NCRNT using art, imagery, historic photographs, documents and interactive elements. The exhibit explores pivotal moments in history from the Nisenan perspective whilst also presenting narratives of a ‘future history’ whereby NCRNT rights and sovereignty are protected and affirmed. [77]

Importantly for the NCRNT, the benefits of such visibility extend beyond community awareness of Nisenan history and support for the Tribe’s ongoing work – VTA has also supported the development of relationships between the NCRNT and local government actors, which have been essential to the growth of NCRNT political agency. [78] [Saxon video 6:51] In 2022, the neighboring city of Grass Valley in Nevada County adopted a resolution codifying their support for the restoration of the Tribe’s federal recognition. In the same year, Nevada City created a permanent position of City Liaison to the Tribal Council, representing the first government-to-government relationship between the Tribe and the city since its founding over 175 years ago. Importantly, the liaison role was developed to provide essential inclusion and capacity support for the Tribe, thus directly addressing some of the chronic vulnerabilities that preclude their engagement in local governance. [79] NCRNT Spokesperson Shelly Covert commented on the significance of this step:

“If we were Federally Recognized, the Tribe would have a space in governing within our homelands. But because we remain in a terminated state, we are reliant upon the community and the partnerships we have made over the last fifteen plus years to ensure our perspectives and visions are heard. We still self-govern, but we do it in a void that is usually invisible to the outside world. Bringing this liaison position forward opens the door for some of these old wisdoms to step through.” [80]

The enduring and growing success of CHIRP and its programs are therefore a testament to the NCRNT’s ability to exercise resilience strategies that engage histories of oppression to construct alternative futures rooted in shared healing, political accountability, and reconciliation.  [Sarah and Saxon conversation – 1:11 – 2:55]

b. Settler and Self Recognition through Tribal Archive Development

The NCRNT is in urgent need of documenting and preserving Nisenan culture and history. The Nisenan have always collected and shared their knowledge through oral histories, much of which was undermined or lost through the California genocide, government removal of children, and the destruction of Nisenan social and cultural fabric into the twentieth century. As noted by Ember Amador, the Executive Assistant for CHIRP, “the archiving has been going on for thousands and thousands of years, and then the library got blown up.” As of 2023, there are only a handful of NCRNT Elders who retain memory of the Tribe’s oral history and of the events of the twentieth century. The Tribe has also not had a physical, centralized archive historically, though Spokesperson Shelly Covert, Tribal Chairman Richard Johnson and other members have been collecting materials informally throughout their lives. Materials range from oral histories and written documents to photographs, videos, audio recordings and physical artifacts. Yet without the resources required to develop a formal archive, most of these materials have remained scattered between the homes of Tribal members or located in institutional repositories such as the National Archives in Washington, DC, which remain out of reach to the Tribe. Shelly Covert highlighted the precarity of the situation, noting that “pulling our collective histories together” means that documents, artifacts, and other materials are collected “from under the sofa over here, you know, in somebody's little stack of something, historical documentation from the historical society, somebody's book, [and other] random things over here.” The absence of a formal archive has therefore made knowledge retention, access to knowledge and information, and ultimately cultural preservation difficult for the NCRNT.

In 2018, the NCRNT began participating in the Tribal Recognition Initiative, [81] hosted and coordinated at the Penn Cultural Heritage Center at the University of Pennsylvania Museum of Archaeology and Anthropology, with support from the Andrew W. Mellon Foundation’s “Just Futures Initiative.” The Initiative works with federally unrecognized tribes in their transition journeys to federal recognition and acknowledgement, including by supporting capacity development such as land return. The partnership between the Initiative and the NCRNT has focused on the development of the Tribal archive, primarily in support of the Tribe’s renewed petition for restoration of their federally recognized status. Through the Initiative, Native and non-Native volunteers and research assistants spent three years assembling all materials mentioning the NCRNT [82] from the relevant National Archives in Washington, DC, and San Bruno, CA. Since 2019, the Tribe has also engaged in an ongoing process of assembling materials from the various private collections of Tribal members to ensure the archive reflects all relevant aspects of NCRNT culture and history. Following their acquisition, materials are digitized and catalogued to the item level according to their collection and repository. While the archive is still in its early stages of development, the Tribe plans to have materials organized and accessible within a dedicated online repository by the end of 2023.

The creation of the NCRNT Tribal archive and its employment for internal cultural preservation and federal recognition is an example of both refusal and recognition at play within NCRNT resilience strategies. Firstly, the development of the archive represents a significant source of cultural preservation and ‘self-recognition’ for current and future generations of the NCRNT beyond the purview of the U.S. government. [Saxon and Shelly video – 8:21 – 9:02] By collecting, organizing, and managing access to their materials, the NCRNT can educate future generations about themselves on their own terms, rather than relying on settler education systems that have constructed narratives of shame for being ‘Indian.’ NCRNT Elder Virginia Covert recalled how in eighth grade she was taught that Indians were ‘savages’ by a teacher of hers, who himself was part Indian. Experiences such as Virginia’s reinforce the necessity of tribes being in control of their own identity, culture and history, as well as how these are taught. Importantly, the development of the archive has also meant that materials are available for educating the public, including through the various exhibitions at ‘Uba Seo. The ability to preserve and share their knowledge internally and amongst the public not only provides a source of education on Nisenan history and culture, but it also serves as a source of external credibility for the Tribe on their own terms. As noted by Tribal Spokesperson Shelly Covert, the development of the archive and preservation of historic artifacts in many ways “makes us more real.”

Second, the creation of a Tribal archive enables the NCRNT to make their history and culture legible to government institutions, which provides the Tribe with agency in seeking forms of recognition. Gathering evidence of their history and culture to support their petition for federal recognition has been a primary focus of the Tribe’s archival development process with the Tribal Recognition Initiative. The current process for seeking federal recognition is incredibly onerous and requires tribes to produce significant bodies of evidence of their own existence. [83] In the context of California history where tribes have had to resort to hiding to survive, recognition requirements are predictably difficult to meet, if not impossible in many cases. Reflecting the neoliberal tendency to place the responsibility of resilience on vulnerable populations instead of the state, the NCRNT is forced to gather historical materials that prove their own existence to be eligible for recognition by the state that erased them in the first place. [84] As noted by Brian I. Daniels in his work on the relationship between tribal sovereignty and cultural preservation, “History dangles the promise of future sovereignty.” [85] By collating both Tribal and U.S. government materials within their own archive, and by employing these materials for visibility across multiple realms of their own choosing, the NCRNT has creatively augmented traditional pathways towards recognition. In tandem with their search for U.S. government recognition, the NCRNT has employed its history and culture to forge forms of local, national and international recognition in the interim.

c. Non-Native Collaboration for Strengthening Tribal Capacity

The NCRNT faces particular challenges to its resilience as a result of both its size as a small tribe and the impacts of structural marginalization upon Tribal resources. Most NCRNT members live at or below the poverty line, which creates structural constraints to Tribal activities such as archive development, Tribal education, environmental protection and other aspects of Nisenan resilience. [86] Without the financial resources needed to pay Tribal members for their time and efforts, engaging and supporting members in cultural preservation activities remains challenging. [87] CHIRP and the Tribe’s resources remain insufficient for supporting the full range of activities required for federal recognition or increasing the broader visibility of the Tribe. Addressing these capacity deficits has therefore been a central focus for the NCRNT, which has led to multiple voluntary collaborative efforts between the Tribe and non-Native individuals and groups.

NCRNT collaborations with non-Native individuals have been particularly effective for increasing the financial capacity of CHIRP and the Tribe. For example, the Tribe’s collaboration with a non-Native grant writer in New York has led to over $255,000 in successful grant applications for CHIRP between 2021 and 2023. And in 2023, this collaboration resulted in CHIRP receiving a three-year $1 million ‘Elevate Youth California’ youth substance use disorder grant, which focuses on educating youth around harm reduction and building peer leadership and awareness around environmental and social justice issues. Collaborations have also focused specifically on establishing legal and political momentum towards re-instating the Tribe’s federal recognition. The NCRNT collaboration with the Tribal Recognition Initiative involves weekly meetings with the Penn Cultural Heritage Center’s Dr. Brian I. Daniels, who provides them with advice tailored to their unique circumstances as a tribe subject to the California Rancheria Act. This collaboration is further supported by federal Indian law expert Frank Lawrence, who joins the same weekly calls to provide pro bono legal support. These collaborations have supplemented the Tribe’s capacity gaps and enabled them to grow CHIRP and seek recognition at a pace that is both manageable for the Tribe’s capacity and in line with their unique needs.

The NCRNT also collaborates across different societal levels to garner visibility and posit their history as a source of nuance for issues that affect them. [88] In 2021, the NCRNT collaborated with Dylan Fitzwater, a master’s student at the University of California, Los Angeles, for his thesis research on the politics of wildfire prevention and Nisenan traditional burn practices. Importantly, Fitzwater’s research draws attention to the political realities of government agencies attempting to work with unrecognized tribes to employ traditional ecological knowledge. [89] Similarly, in 2022 the NCRNT collaborated with Aleena Church, a master’s student at the California State Polytechnic University, Humboldt, to produce an understanding of ongoing settler colonial violence against sacred Nisenan water resources. Church worked closely with the Tribe using archival research and Participatory Action Research methods to produce a shared understanding of the Tribe’s history and contemporary realities, which relied upon the use of Nisenan art, poetry and storytelling. [90] And as of 2022, the NCRNT began collaborating with University of Cambridge doctoral candidate Megan Renoir, for her historical research on the Tribe’s institutional relationships vis-à-vis the U.S. government between 1891 and 1964. [91] Renoir’s engagement with the Tribe has included ongoing support for their archive’s development, and research that provides the Tribe with other avenues for developing visibility.

In all cases, the NCRNT research collaborations with non-Native students have provided the Tribe with additional capacity to facilitate the production of research that reflects the Indigenous reality as well as a settler reality, and that reframes damaging historical narratives to support NCRNT resilience. Because each researcher has engaged with the historical experiences of the NCRNT, these research collaborations have also led to the Tribe receiving continued support for furthering the development of their archive.

Moreover, research collaborations have provided the NCRNT with access to visibility that facilitates Tribal resilience. The NCRNTs partnership with CDA Collaborative Learning regarding Indigenous resilience to conflict and climate change has offered the Tribe a platform for visibility beyond the local and national context. As noted by Lindroth and Sinevaara-Niskanen however, one risk of these types of projects is that they represent the necessity of Indigenous groups accommodating themselves to “[settler] political and legal frameworks by constantly offering evidence that validates their existence and causes.” [92] This risk is of course reflected in the evidence requirements that the Tribe must meet in its fight for federal recognition. It is also not hard to see how the NCRNT research collaboration with CDA could reflect the same risk. By working with CDA to provide examples of resilience strategies that can inform the work of global development practitioners, the NCRNT are offering evidence that reinforces their existence and legitimacy at the national and international level. This is not necessarily a destructive process for groups like the NCRNT. [93] As a small unrecognized nation, such collaborations and visibility can lead to significant opportunities for external capacity support outside of the U.S. government, which may therefore provide the Tribe with other alternatives to recognition. [94] The collaboration with CDA also highlights the value and significance of engaging with Indigenous histories for informing understandings of resilience. By collaborating with CDA and sharing their unique experience of resilience at the local, national, and global level, the NCRNT provided practical evidence to inform normative shifts in the concept and practical application of resilience within international development.  As a result, and in the absence of federal recognition, the NCRNT has therefore deployed its own visibility as a method for constructing and implementing an alternative concept of resilience that reflects its historic oppression and the ideals of a ‘new normal.’

Conclusion

This examination of the Nevada City Rancheria Nisenan Tribe’s resilience strategies reveals the necessity of framing resilience through histories of oppression and the concept of restorative justice. The NCRNT’s experience reflects the flaws of existing resilience frameworks that focus narrowly on ‘bouncing back’ without addressing root causes of marginalization. For the NCRNT, there is no ‘normal’ state to return to after two centuries of genocide, land dispossession, and denial of sovereignty. The Tribe’s goal is not recovery but forging new futures that enable NCRNT revival through visibility. By creatively leveraging cultural preservation efforts, arts, and non-Native collaboration, the NCRNT has augmented constrained pathways for recognition. This multi-pronged approach strengthens internal Tribal preservation, non-Native relations, and governance partnerships, while seeking U.S. acknowledgement as a parallel process. The NCRNT’s self-defined resilience, centered on visibility and reciprocal healing, provides a model for how international development actors can expand rigid resilience frameworks focused on short-term stabilization. The NCRNT resilience “strategy” reveals how marginalized communities carve space for agency when formal structures are inaccessible. By making their erasure visible across various realms, the NCRNT shapes visibility into a pathway for reasserting identity, rights, and collective futures. Their approach points to the emancipatory potential of visibility as a critical element of Indigenous self-determination and sovereignty within international development.
